Timeline

When I formed a UX team to collaborate with the UC San Diego Library, we were intially tasked with researching how users were accessing help on the Library website. The project evolved into a design project where we brought together virtual and physical interactions to inform the Library of better practices toward effective service delivery.

I had the great opportunity to formalize and spearhead this project, provided to UC San Diego's student design agency, Fi@UCSD.

Pain points

What can we learn from users and the state of help on the Library site to improve help delivery?

I directed my team to start by collecting user insights through 1) in-person interviews and 2) an online survey to understand how users interact with Library services and learn how users prefer getting help, respectively. The team also performed an expert review to determine usability issues with the Library's help page ("Ask Us").

3. Users prefer methods of help outside of the Library's online services

On the same survey, participants were asked what help channels they would prefer to use in different scenarios on the Library site.

In general, users preferred using the Library's search function, Googling, or asking in person, to get help over all of the available help services on the Library website.

We used our responses and feedback to conclude that online services on the Library site are often perceived as

inconvenient, inefficient, slow, and counter-intuitive.

The next step we had was to discover why.

4. Help functionality on the Library site isn't effective

Our team's expert review revealed several issues with the flow and usability of the Library's online help services.

These findings were collated into a report and provided more comprehensive feedback on why the Ask Us page was described as not helpful by several users.

Solution scope

How do we use discovered pain points to propose new ideas for solutions?

Our job in this project wasn't to propose designs, but rather ideas that would inform design decisions for the Library UX team and the respective 300+ staff when delivering help service to Library users.

I joined my co-PM in looking at our user insights and usability analysis to create a new scope. This scope would encompass how any solution, created by the team or by the Library client group, should revolve around when targeting better help effectiveness and efficiency.

1. Graduate students and faculty reported that finding help on the website for their needs is troublesome and time-consuming.

Opening discoverable help functions across the site allows users to access relevant and helpful feedback to complete the task they need to do, while also engaging users with help services.

2. Undergraduate students reported that it was often hard to understand the services offered by the Library.

Creating frequent tips and dialogue on resource-specific pages helps the user to successfully utilize that resource and will increase success rates for a given task.

3. The "Ask Us" page was flagged in the expert review as highly inefficient in motivating a user to use advertised help services.

Recreating the Ask Us page as a central hub for finding help, with effective display of information and quick actions for getting help, is the easiest solution to implement to resolve issues in finding help.

4. Search was the most frequently preferred method of finding help, but suffers from effectiveness and prominence on the Library site.

In contrast to design, there is a technical need to rethink how a new search implementation would play a role in the virtual service model of the Library. Ideally, a new search engine would deliver more relevant results and a brighter interface.

5. Users desire to recognize the specialties of librarians and the breadth of their knowledge to help users answer their questions.

Creating the same level of engagement in interactions in cohesive physical and virtual service models provides useful metrics for empowering service usage.

For the purpose of workload and time, I directed the team to specialize on an easy solution. Through task management, I split the team into subteams, focusing their design efforts on the third solution we scoped: rethinking the "Ask Us" page to be a hub for help.

This required an extensive competitive analysis, user flowcharts, and a co-design session with users to investigate potential solutions. For more detail on the work done in solution research, refer to pages 14-18 of the UX audit report we created here.

Ideation

What can we do to learn more from users and other solutions to form our very own?

With specification of what our team would focus on in terms of help delivery, I directed our team to ideate through more research. This effort was focused directly on looking to other solutions (competitor sites), optimizing flow of user tasks, and co-designing directly with users.

Competitive Analysis

A competitive feature analysis allowed our team to think more deeply into what other academic library sites were doing to service their users. We looked through 22 different library websites, looking for web component features that would prove useful to the UCSD¬†Library site.

Ask Us redesign proposal

How can we use our research collectively to propose new ways of finding help?

The designs from the team were created to renovate the old Ask Us page, addressing usability issues found in our expert review while also proposing new features formulated through discovering pain points from users. I was able to take those designs and communicate added features into our final presentation.

Which one looks better?
I should really ask which one is more legible from your point of view.

Horizontal grid for interactive help services

We utilized the horizontal space to minimize clutter and encourage users to interact directly with the different help channels.

Users can hover to find numbers for phone help services, and click on chat, email, and consultation services to directly send a request without being redirected to other pages.

New layout for Guides

Guides for getting started are bucketed into categories for quick parsing and access by user's demographic, department, or community.

New search functionality

Users can utilize a more effective search to continue searching for Library resources versus going back to the homepage.
